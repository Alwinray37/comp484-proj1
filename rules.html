Rules & Gameplay

Volleyball is a fast-paced sport with a clear structure. Whether you're watching for the first time or stepping onto the court, understanding the rules makes all the difference.

A volleyball match is divided into sets, and each set is a race to reach a target score.

Sets & Winning a Match

Most matches are played as a best-of-five sets format. The first team to win three sets wins the match. In shorter competitions, a best-of-three format (first to two sets) is also common.

How Sets Are Scored

Sets 1 through 4 are played to 25 points, and a team must win by at least 2 points. If the score reaches 24–24, play continues until one team leads by two. If a fifth (deciding) set is needed, it is played to only 15 points, again with a 2-point margin required to win.

Set Scoring at a Glance

Set Points to Win Win-By Margin
Sets 1 – 4 25 2
Set 5 (deciding) 15 2

Rally Scoring

Modern volleyball uses rally scoring, which means every single rally results in a point — no matter who served.

How It Works

A rally begins with a serve and ends when the ball touches the floor, goes out of bounds, or a team commits a fault. Whichever team wins the rally earns one point and the right to serve next. This system keeps the game moving quickly and ensures every play matters.

Serving Order & Rotation

When a team wins back the serve from the opponent, all six of its players rotate one position clockwise before the next serve. This ensures every player takes a turn serving and playing in each position on the court. Serving out of order results in a fault and loss of the point.

Core Rules of Play

These are the fundamental rules that govern every rally in indoor volleyball.

The Three-Hit Rule

Each team is allowed a maximum of three contacts with the ball before sending it over the net. A typical sequence is a pass (bump), a set, and then an attack (spike). A block touch does not count as one of the three contacts.

Legal Contacts

Players may use any part of their body to contact the ball. The ball must be hit cleanly — it cannot be caught, held, or carried. Double contacts (hitting the ball twice in a row with one motion) are illegal, except on a first contact when the ball arrives with force (such as on a serve receive or a hard spike).

Net & Boundary Rules

Players may not touch the net during play. The ball is considered in-bounds if it lands on or inside the boundary lines. Players may reach over the net to block a ball, but only after the attacking team has made their final contact. The ball may also be played off the net and remain in play.

Serving Rules

The server must stand behind the end line when serving. The serve may be underhand or overhand, and jump serves are also allowed. The serve must clear the net without being touched by a teammate. If the serve hits the net but still lands in-bounds, it is legal and play continues (a "net serve").

Common Faults

A fault results in the opposing team winning the rally and earning a point.

Faults That End a Rally

The most common faults in volleyball include: hitting the ball out of bounds, failing to send the ball over the net, a player touching the net, a team using more than three contacts, a player committing a double hit (outside of a first-contact exception), and a player crossing under the net and interfering with the opponent. Foot faults on the serve — stepping on or over the end line before contact — are also called regularly.

Fault Result

Timeouts & Substitutions

Teams have limited opportunities to regroup and make changes during a set.

Timeouts

Each team is allowed two timeouts per set, each lasting 30 seconds. Coaches use timeouts to adjust strategy, break an opponent's momentum, or give players a brief rest. In televised matches, additional technical timeouts may be called automatically at set score milestones.

Substitutions

Teams are allowed up to six substitutions per set. A player who is substituted out may return, but only in the same position they left. The libero — a specialized defensive player — is subject to separate substitution rules and may freely rotate in and out for back-row players without using the team's substitution limit.
